Dear friend,

If you've landed here you've probably already been around. You've clicked through agency sites talking about digital transformation, freelancer portfolios with stock photography, and SaaS pricing pages promising the $149-a-month plan will finally hold it all. I won't do that to you. This is a letter, not a sales page — and I'd rather be honest with you about how a small studio actually works.

I'm Jordi. I live in Mazunte, a small village on the Pacific coast of Oaxaca, with my partner Nina and our two children. I spent ten years before this building production systems and leading engineering teams in fintech and SaaS, the last few as CTO of a venture-backed startup. Then I moved here, and started building, slowly, for the kind of people I actually wanted to work with — artists, healers, practitioners, retreat-leaders, community-keepers, the founders of schools that don't quite fit on Teachable.

I want to tell you what the studio does, why we hold the work this way, and how a conversation with us begins.

One · what we do

Our role is not to invent the work. It is to support what is already real with structure that fits.

We work with projects that already exist — communities, practitioners, programs, spaces, initiatives — and we build the digital home that meets them. Often that means replacing four or five rented tools (Calendly, Teachable, Stripe, Mailchimp, a Squarespace site, a Linktree) with one custom platform on a single domain, wired together from the first commit, yours to own from day one. Sometimes it means something narrower: a quiet bespoke site for a maker, a directory for a town, a private membership for a teacher with a real methodology.

Every site we build is custom. No templates, no themes, no page builders. Real code, your brand, your domain, your data. If we ever stop working together you walk away with the keys.

Two · the work

A few of the platforms we currently hold:

Sacred Counsel, a retreat center with twenty years of practice running five distinct streams (retreats, integration therapy, homestays, a 21-day metabolic program) — unified into one platform with stream-specific intake flows and capacity logic underneath. Mazunte Today, the bilingual community platform for our own town: event listings, practitioner directory, weekly digest, in two languages from day one.

Amakura, a multidisciplinary collective in Zapotal — school of regenerative agriculture, bioconstruction, arts & crafts — brought into a single digital home that reflects the land. Inner Ascend, a private membership for a practitioner with a coherent methodology for inner development — progression-based practice, an assistant trained on her language, a pipeline into facilitator training.

Proyecto Salvaje, a regenerative neighborhood ten minutes from here. Mend a Mano, a textile artisan whose bespoke linen robes deserved a site that felt like the cloth. Portal Poem, a writer's daily-prompt practice held in a paper-aesthetic editorial home. Kura Terra, plant-dyed slow fashion presented as wearable art.

You can click any of those to see them living on the open web. The list is short on purpose: we hold a small number of engagements at any time, and we stay with each one for the long arc of the work.

Three · how we work

We are a small studio. There are no account managers and no junior contractors. The person who reads your first message is the person who will write your code, and they are also the person who will still be there in two years when you want to add a curriculum engine or a second language. This is the unusual part.

Most agencies build and disappear. Most freelancers stay but build on rented foundations. Most platforms give you templates and own everything you make on them. We try to be the last technical hire you'll need — the one who builds it cleanly, hands you the keys, and stays alongside you as the work grows.

That's only sustainable because we are selective. We take eight or ten engagements at a time. We say no often. When the fit is right, we treat each project as a relationship that lasts as long as the practice does — which is usually longer than any single platform.

Four · how it begins

Before any build, before any proposal, every project begins with the same first step:

/ The first step

The Digital Realignment Review.

A grounded look at your existing landscape — website, offerings, tools, structure, language, flow. We read what already exists. We identify where meaning or value is unclear. We tell you, plainly, what your work is currently communicating and where the structure does not support the intention.

You receive it as a written review or a Loom walkthrough — whichever serves the work better. It stands on its own; many people take what they learn and implement it themselves. There is no obligation to continue.

Investment $450
Timeline 5–7 days
Format Written or Loom

For projects that move forward, we agree the build and the partnership in conversation — sized to your stage, your scope, and your work. I won't list a rate card here because the right number for a textile maker selling slowly is not the right number for a school running three certifications across two languages, and the right number for either is something we should talk about together rather than read off a menu.

What we commit to in advance: no surprises, no change orders, no compounding monthly platform fees. Whatever the engagement looks like, you own the code, the data, the domain, and the customer relationships from the first day forward.

Five · the part I mean

Here's the part that doesn't go in pitches.

The people who come to you are looking for something their doctor and their therapist and their meditation app cannot give them. Presence. Attention. The transmission of a lineage that is not available on Spotify. Whether that is a single bespoke garment, a daily writing practice, a multi-level training, or a six-day retreat — what you do is not a product, and it does not belong on a page that pretends it is.

Your digital presence should match the work. Not in a slick, VC-funded-wellness-startup way. In a way that carries the actual texture of your teaching, or your craft, or your community, when a stranger lands on your page at two in the morning.

That is what the studio holds. That is what we are paid to build.
