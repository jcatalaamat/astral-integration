01

We build the infrastructure for real-world work.

Our role is not to invent the work — it is to support what is already real with structure that fits.

You inspire hundreds in a weekend, then spend three days fighting five disconnected tools — Calendly for bookings, Teachable for courses, Stripe for checkout, Mailchimp for the list, WhatsApp for the community. Six platforms, six brands, none of them yours.

That is not a digital presence — it is digital fragmentation. Every link sends people out of your world and into someone else's. The most valuable thing you have is the relationship with your people, and it is being held hostage by software that was never built for the work.

We work with projects that already exist — communities, practitioners, programs, spaces, initiatives — and support them in clarifying how the work is expressed, accessed, and sustained digitally. We step in as a clarifying and structuring layer: read what already exists, identify what matters now, and help it take a form that others can understand and engage with.

We build systems, not one-off deliverables. We prioritize clarity before execution. We work lean, with a small core and trusted collaborators. We favor ownership and shared upside over hourly billing. We work remotely and asynchronously.

From London to Barcelona to Mexico City — and, finally, to a small village on the Oaxacan coast.

Jordi is from Catalonia. He spent a decade building production systems and leading engineering teams in fintech and SaaS — first as a senior full-stack engineer, then as CTO of a venture-backed startup. He shipped real software to real users, hired and led teams, and learned what it takes to turn a methodology into infrastructure that holds.

In 2022 he moved to Mazunte, a small village on the coast of Oaxaca, Mexico, with his partner Nina and their two children. This isn't a digital nomad chapter — it's home. They chose this place for the community, the land, and the kind of life they want their kids to grow up in.

Half the village are practitioners, facilitators, artists, and builders of alternative schools and projects. He saw the same pattern everywhere: brilliant people running powerful work on scattered tools that were never built for them. So he started Astral Studio — a small, focused studio building the infrastructure that meets the work.

He has trained in Access Bars, Theta Healing, Reiki, Gene Keys, Family Constellations, Ashtanga, Vinyasa, Yin, Kundalini, transpersonal work, psychedelic integration, ceremony, holding space — not because he planned to build platforms for the people doing this work, but because the work called. Each training changed how he understands what artists, creatives, changemakers, practitioners, facilitators, and organizations actually need from their infrastructure.

The Linktree Problem Field note · 4 min · Infrastructure

Why linking to six platforms isn't a digital strategy.

Your Linktree has six links. Calendly for bookings. Teachable for courses. Stripe for payments. A Squarespace site. A WhatsApp group. A Mailchimp form. Every click sends people out of your world and into someone else's. Your booking platform doesn't know who took your course. Your course platform doesn't know who's on your email list. You have no unified view of the people you serve — just fragments scattered across platforms that were never designed to talk to each other.

The practitioners we work with typically have between 3 and 8 platforms in their stack. The shift isn't complicated: replace all of them with one custom platform that does exactly what you need, under your brand, with your data, in one place.

Field note · 5 min

Then we built one platform that understands what a certification school actually is — prerequisite-gated progression, hour tracking, automatic practitioner directory. Five platforms became one. The spreadsheet disappeared."
