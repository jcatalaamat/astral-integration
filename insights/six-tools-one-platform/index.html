Six Tools, One Platform

What changes when you stop paying rent on other people's software

Sera runs a Reiki school in Granada with 300+ students across 9 certification levels. When we first talked, her digital infrastructure looked like this: Teachable for course delivery. Calendly for session bookings. Stripe for payments. WhatsApp for student communication. Google Sheets for tracking who had completed what level and how many practice hours they'd logged.

Five platforms. None of them talked to each other. None of them knew she was running a certification school with prerequisite levels.

Every time a student completed Level 3, Sera had to manually check their practice hours in a spreadsheet, verify prerequisites in Teachable, and then send them a WhatsApp message telling them they could enroll in Level 4. If a student asked "what's my next step?", the answer required cross-referencing three platforms.

The monthly cost of the five platforms was around $200. The real cost — the hours Sera spent every week on admin instead of teaching — was far higher.

Here's what we built: a single platform that understands what a Reiki certification school actually is. Course delivery with prerequisite-gated progression, so Level 4 only unlocks when Level 3 and the required practice hours are complete. Student tracking across all 9 levels with practice hour logging. Automatic population of a public practitioner directory when someone reaches certification. Booking for retreat sessions. An AI assistant trained on the Reiki methodology.

Five platforms became one. The spreadsheet disappeared. Booking confirmations are automatic. Students can see their entire journey — from first course to practitioner certification — in one place.

Sera's monthly platform costs went from $200 across five tools to zero platform fees (the custom platform runs on infrastructure that costs a fraction of what she was paying). More importantly, she got back the hours she was spending on admin every single week.

This isn't about technology. It's about building infrastructure that matches the work. A certification school needs certification logic — prerequisites, progression tracking, practice hour requirements. Teachable doesn't have that because Teachable was built for selling video courses, not running schools.

The tools you're paying for were designed for a different kind of business. The question isn't which tool to switch to. It's whether to stop renting and build a home.
